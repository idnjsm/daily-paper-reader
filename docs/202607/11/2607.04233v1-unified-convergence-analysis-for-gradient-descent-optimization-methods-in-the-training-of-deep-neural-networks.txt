Abstract 

Gradient based optimization methods are nowadays the methods of choice for training deep neural networks (DNNs) in artificial intelligence (AI) systems. In practically relevant DNN training problems, one does usually not apply the standard gradient descent (GD) optimization method but instead one employs suitable sophisticated GD optimization methods, which incorporate adaptivity and/or acceleration techniques, such as the famous Adam optimizer. It is a key contribution of this work to provide a general unified convergence analysis for GD optimization methods in the training of DNNs with analytic activations such as the softplus and the popular Gaussian error linear unit (GeLU) activation. Our general unified convergence result applies to a large class of gradient based optimization methods such as the standard GD, the momentum, the Nesterov accelerated gradient (NAG), the RMSprop, the Adam, the Adamax, the Nadam, the Nadamax, the Adan, the AdaBelief, the AMSGrad, and the Yogi optimizers. Our analysis employs the theory of Kurdyka-Lojasiewicz (KL) inequalities to establish convergence to critical points in the training of DNNs. To the best of our knowledge, the generality of our convergence analysis is also just in the special situation of the Adam optimizer a new contribution to the literature on the analysis of AI optimization algorithms.

Gradient descent (GD) based optimization methods are nowadays the standard methods for the training of deep neural networks (DNNs) in artificial intelligence (AI) systems. In the training of large scale AI models, the standard GD method and its stochastic counterpart [26], respectively, are often not the employed optimization methods but instead suitable sophisticated variants of the standard GD method involving adaptivity and acceleration are considered [33, 54]. Prominent adaptive and/or accelerated GD based optimization methods are, for instance, (i) the momentum [50], (ii) the Nesterov accelerated gradient (NAG) [49], (iii) the root mean square propagation (RMSprop) [31], (iv) the adaptive moment estimation (Adam) [38], (v) the adaptive moment estimation maximum (Adamax) [38], (vi) the Nesterov accelerated adaptive moment estimation (Nadam) [23], (vii) the Nesterov accelerated adaptive moment estimation maximum (Nadamax) [23], (viii) the vanilla adaptive Nesterov momentum (Adan) [63], (ix) the adaptive belief (AdaBelief) [71], (x) the AMSGrad [53], and (xi) the Yogi [67] optimizers. The most popular of such optimization methods is presumably the Adam optimizer (see item (iv) above) proposed in 2014 by Kingma & Ba [38]. Despite the popularity and the success of Adam, it remained an open question to prove (or disprove) strong convergence of every bounded Adam optimization trajectory in the training of DNNs. In this work we partially solve this problem by establishing – in the training of fully-connected feedforward DNNs with analytic activations – convergence with convergence rates not only for every bounded optimization trajectory of Adam but for each of the above mentioned optimizers (i)–(xi). Specifically, it is a key contribution of the main result of this work, see Theorem 3.9 in Subsection 3.3 below, to develop a unified framework 

(a) which includes each of the above mentioned optimization methods (i)–(xi) as a special case and (b) under which every bounded trajectory of the optimizer converges with convergence rates to a critical point for general Kurdyka- Lojasiewicz (KL) objective functions with locally Lipschitz continuous gradients. In Definition 3.1 in Subsection 3.1 below we recall the notion of a KL function (cf., for example, [2, Definition 1], [3, Definition 9], and [33, Definition 9.1.2]). KL objective functions cover, for instance, the training of DNNs with analytic activation functions as a special case; see Subsection 3.4 and Corollary 1.2 below for details. ## 1.3 Literature overview 

In this subsection we briefly review selected further results from the literature that theoretically analyze GD optimization algorithms, where we particularly focus on convergence results for accelerated and adaptive GD optimization methods. 

Adaptive and accelerated GD optimization methods beyond Adam 

Since [53] has demonstrated that Adam can fail to converge even on simple one-dimensional convex objectives, a proliferation of Adam variants has emerged. Weak convergence – conver-gence of function values at the iterates – has been established for convex objective functions in methods such as AdaBelief [71], FastAdaBelief [70], and SAdam [59]. Relaxing convexity, anal-yses of variants like AdamL [61] achieve weak convergence by assuming the Polyak- Lojasiewicz 

(PL) condition. In broader non-convex regimes, weak convergence proofs are achieved assum-ing (lower) boundedness of the objective function and/or Lipschitz continuous gradients, as demonstrated in the analyses of Adan [63], Yogi [67], AdaBound [41, 48], AMSBound [48], SignSGD [7], generalized SignSGD [12], AdaNorm [24, 25], AdaGrad [13, 32, 58], and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with momentum and difference (SGD (MD)) [66]. Beyond weak guar-antees, strong convergence – convergence of the iterates themselves – has been established in highly restricted settings, such as for quadratic objective functions in, for instance, AdaLoss [60]. Moreover, other variants of Adam have been introduced based purely on numerical simulations and empirical performance (see, for example, RAdam [43] and EAdam [65]). Beyond adaptive optimizers, foundational algorithms such as SGD and its momentum-based extensions – including the Heavy-Ball (HB) and NAG methods – have been extensively analyzed. However, for general smooth objectives, the established theoretical guarantees pre-dominantly remain weak. In non-convex settings, weak convergence and associated rates have been established for SGD [68] and momentum GD [42,44,64] under bounded variance and stan-dard or generalized Lipschitz continuous gradients. Similarly, under global convexity, analyses of the HB method provide weak convergence guarantees in both stochastic and deterministic regimes [28, 56]. Strong convergence for these foundational momentum algorithms is largely confined to highly restricted structural settings. For instance, (global) linear strong convergence for mo-mentum methods is established, but for convex quadratic objectives, such as least-squares regression problems [45, 46]. Comparable strong guarantees and lower error bounds for stan-dard SGD also rely on quadratic landscapes [35], or demand rigid structural constraints when applied to broader convex functions [55]. To bridge the gap to strong convergence in more general non-convex landscapes, recent analyses have increasingly leveraged geometric properties such as the PL and KL conditions. Strong convergence of deterministic momentum GD to a local minimizer of semialgebraic ob-jective functions (which inherently satisfy the KL property) is established in [37] with constant hyperparameters. However, this relies on restrictive assumptions regarding the Hessian of the objective function – specifically imposing the strict saddle property by requiring a strictly neg-ative eigenvalue at every critical point that is not a local minimum point. Moreover, strong 6convergence of SGD has been proven for analytic functions satisfying the KL property [57], while momentum SGD has been investigated under both PL [27] and KL [22, 52] geometries. Furthermore, [5] established strong convergence for a generalized class of stochastic momentum methods which encompasses Adam. However, these strong convergence results enforce coupling between the momentum decay parameters ( αn)n∈N ⊆ [0 , 1] and non-summable learning rates (γn)n∈N ⊆ (0 , ∞), for instance, setting αn = 1 − rnγn for some ( rn)n∈N ⊆ (0 , ∞) in [5,22,27] and 

αn = λγ n−1(γn)−1 for arbitrary λ ∈ [0 , 1) in [52]. Such coupled restrictions limit their general-ity, in contrast to our deterministic framework, which accommodates arbitrary hyperparameter decoupling. 

Convergence analyses for the Adam optimizer 

Under standard assumptions of (lower) boundedness of the objective function and/or Lipschitz continuous gradients, weak convergence – typically formulated as bounds for the gradient or the objective function evaluated at the iterates – has been widely established for Adam [11, 13, 30, 69,72] and unified Adam-type frameworks [36]. Weak convergence bounds for Adam have been derived under generalized ( L0, L ρ)-smoothness conditions [40], and frameworks encompassing the broader Adam family have been analyzed in non-smooth landscapes using subdifferentials [62]. Strong convergence for adaptive gradient methods remains rare and is confined to highly restricted structural settings. For example, local strong convergence for Adam has been estab-lished, but only within local regions where the objective function behaves as strongly convex (exhibiting a positive definite Hessian) [9, 21]. Scaling to global guarantees, recent uncondi-tional error analyses [14] have secured strong convergence for Adam, but require the objective function to be globally strongly convex. Similarly, while [29] establishes strong convergence for SGD with adaptive scaling matrix ( An)n∈N – similar to our formulation in (1.2) in Theo-rem 1.1 when pn = 0 or |pn − 1| + α + ∥μn∥ = 0 for all n ∈ N – using smallest eigenvalue control lim inf n→∞ λmin (An) > 0, their analysis is restricted to quasi-strongly convex objective functions. Seeking broader generality beyond strict convexity, Barakat & Bianchi investigated Adam, initially deriving weak convergence for an objective function with a Lipschitz continuous gra-dient [3], and subsequently proving strong convergence under the KL property to a critical point via an associated ordinary differential equation (ODE) limit [4]. While decaying learning rates are a fundamental necessity for achieving stochastic convergence – as demonstrated by non-convergence results for non-decaying learning rate schedules even for quadratic optimiza-tion [16] – achieving strong convergence via continuous-time ODE limits [4], and the afore-mentioned weak convergence results [36, 40, 62], additionally require the first-order momentum decay parameters ( αn)n∈N ⊆ [0 , 1] to converge to 1 or to be coupled with non-summable learn-ing rates ( γn)n∈N ⊆ (0 , ∞), setting, for example, αn = 1 − rnγn for some ( rn)n∈N ⊆ (0 , ∞). Consequently, an analysis that secures strong convergence for Adam in general non-convex KL landscapes – without, for instance, chaining the momentum hyperparameters to the learning rate – remained an open problem that our unified framework resolved. 

## 1.4 Structure of this article 

The remainder of this work is organized as follows. In Section 2 we establish under suitable assumptions within a unified framework, which we refer to as UGD approach, convergence of the evaluation of the objective function at the considered optimization process to a critical level of the objective function (to the evaluation of the objective function at a critical point of the objective function); see Corollary 2.9 for the main result of Section 2. In Section 3 we employ this weak convergence result for the UGD method from Corollary 2.9 to establish for every 7bounded optimization trajectory of the UGD method strong convergence to a critical point for general KL objective functions with locally Lipschitz continuous gradients; see Corollary 3.6, Theorem 3.9, Corollary 3.10, and Corollary 3.11. Moreover, in Subsection 3.4 we illustrate the conclusion of the strong convergence result in Corollary 3.11 in the situation of the training of DNNs. Finally, in Section 4 we apply Corollary 3.11 to several concrete optimization methods such as each of the optimizers in (i)–(xi) above.

# 3 Strong convergence for GD optimization methods 

In this section we establish in Theorem 3.9 below the main result of this work, in which we develop a unified framework which includes each of the optimization methods (i)–(xi) from Section 1 as a special case and under which every bounded trajectory of the optimizer con-verges with convergence rates to a critical point for general KL objective functions with locally Lipschitz continuous gradients. In Corollary 3.11 we reformulate and specialize Theorem 3.9 to the situation where the momentum decay factors do not depend on the number of time steps and in Section 4 we apply Corollary 3.11 to each of the optimization methods (i)–(xi) from Section 1. The arguments in 17 our proof of Proposition 3.2 are in parts inspired by the arguments, for instance, in the proofs of [1, Theorem 3.2] and [34, Proposition 8.1].
